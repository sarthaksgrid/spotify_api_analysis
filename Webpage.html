Beyond the Beats: What Spotify’s Data Really Says About Your Playlists

Ever wondered if your "Happy" playlist is actually making you happy, or if "Sad" labels are more about stillness than heartbreak?

In the world of music streaming, sound is transformed into data through numerical scores like Energy and Valence, a measure of musical positiveness, to organise tracks into mood-based genres.

This analysis explores whether these labels reflect genuine musical emotion or primarily encode physical intensity, targeting curators and analysts who rely on audio features to infer mood.

Spotify largely defines these categories in physical and rhythmic terms. The "Happy" genre is characterised by extreme Energy and high tempo, focusing on loud, fast, and physically "busy" tracks.

Conversely, the "Sad" genre captures music with low Loudness and minimal rhythmic attack, prioritising stillness and lack of movement over actual emotional negativity.

Using a dataset of over 114,000 tracks, we evaluate these categories as algorithmic classifications within Spotify’s feature space.

By comparing their emotional and physical profiles against a multi-genre baseline, we identify where these labels align with human perception and where the algorithmic definitions begin to break down.

Engineering a Clean Dataset

To ensure a fair comparison, we applied a multi-stage cleaning process to the raw dataset extracted via the Spotify Web API.

This removed noise and inconsistencies, producing a high-quality analytical sample of 7,879 tracks and ensuring that observed patterns reflect Spotify’s labelling logic rather than data artefacts.

We focused on 12 core audio features, prioritising Valence (tonal positiveness) and Energy/Loudness (physical intensity), which directly align with the research question.

Over 24,000 duplicate tracks were removed to avoid skewed aggregates, and a baseline control group of 11 diverse genres, including Acoustic, Jazz, Classical, and Pop, was established to provide context beyond isolated genre comparisons.

Genre names were standardised and incomplete records removed to ensure consistency.

This preparation cleanly separates emotional tone from physical intensity, providing a reliable foundation for interpretation and downstream recommendations.

Part 1: The "Happy" Genre Paradox: Is Sunshine Actually Shadows?

We started with a simple check: does a song in the "Happy" genre actually sound positive?

To answer this, we used Valence, Spotify’s measure of tonal positiveness derived from musical features such as key, harmony, and rhythm.

While Valence is not a direct measure of human emotion, it provides a consistent and scalable proxy for comparing tonal mood across large samples.

The Question: Does the Label Match the Mood?

We expected songs labeled as "Happy" to lead the pack in positivity. However, the data revealed an interesting mismatch.

1. The Mood Mountain

The gold peak (Happy genre) is shifted significantly to the left, crowding into the negative-mood zone, while other music is more balanced suggesting that the label "Happy" is quite misleading when it comes to the actual tonal positiveness of the tracks: the music is physically fast, but tonally dark.

2. The Energy Paradox

We brought in Energy (intensity and speed) to see if we were getting an anomaly due to the tempo.

Here is the major contrast we see: "Happy" tracks represent a massive paradox, boasting near-perfect Energy (0.91)—nearly twice the intensity of the average music library —while falling 31.7% behind in actual tonal positiveness with an average Valence of 0.33 compared to the 0.48 baseline.

This comparison emphasises effect size and the direction of the difference across distributions, confirming that the label primarily encodes physical intensity rather than positive musical emotion.

3. The Happy Fingerprint

“Happy” tracks cluster tightly in a high- Energy, low-Valence region, indicating that the label is driven more by physical intensity than by tonal positiveness.

The Verdict: Intensity Over Emotion

The evidence shows that Spotify’s “Happy” label reflects high physical intensity rather than positive emotional tone. “Happy” tracks consistently cluster in high- Energy, low- Valence regions, indicating an intensity-driven classification.

Takeaway: For music that actually sounds sunny and upbeat, prioritise high Valence scores over the “Happy” tag.

Part 2: The Illusion of "Sad" Music : Is it Actually Depressing?

After discovering that the "Happy" genre is more about intensity than mood, we turned our focus to the "Sad" genre. Does this label actually deliver the musical blues, or is it another case of data-label mismatch?

The Question: Is "Sad" Music Just "Slow" Music?

We wanted to see if songs labeled "Sad" are actually tonally negative (Low Valence) or if they are simply characterised by low energy levels.

1. The Emotional Valley

“Sad” tracks lean toward lower Valence but do not concentrate in extreme negative ranges.

Unlike the “Happy” label mismatch, the Sad genre remains close to the emotional baseline, primarily avoiding high-euphoria peaks rather than expressing consistently negative mood.

2. The "Sad" Profile

If the mood isn't that different, what is? We looked at the combination of Energy and Valence.

The real difference is found in Energy, as tracks labeled "Sad" show a modest reduction in average Valence compared to the library (0.44 vs. 0.46, ~5%).

This limited separation indicates that the "Sad" label remains close to the emotional baseline rather than expressing extreme negative tone.

Instead, these songs are primarily characterised by physical intensity, sitting roughly 11% lower in energy than the average music library. They are consistently quieter and slower, creating a profile of calm rather than a profile of depression.

3. The Sad Fingerprint

Finally, we mapped the "Sad" tracks to see where they live on the intensity-mood map.

“Sad” tracks concentrate in a low- Energy, lower- Valence region, but with a broad distribution that extends into moderate Valence and Energy ranges.

Unlike the tightly clustered “Happy” genre, this spread indicates that “Sad” functions as a broad, low-intensity label, covering music that avoids arousal rather than consistently expressing strong negative emotion.

The Verdict: Sadness is a "Physical Slowdown"

In Spotify’s system, the “Sad” label reflects low physical intensity more than emotional distress. Unlike the misleading “Happy” tag, it is largely accurate, capturing quieter, slower tracks that avoid high-energy peaks.

Takeaway: Use the “Sad” tag as a reliable filter for calm, low-energy music. For genuinely depressing tracks, filter for ultra-low Valence (< 0.2).

Interpreting the Results: Variance and Trade-Offs

While the observed Valence differences are statistically detectable given the sample size, the substantial overlap in distributions highlights high within-genre variance.

This study identifies a correlation between high-intensity physical markers and these tags, but it avoids causal claims regarding listener sentiment, noting that descriptive statistics are sufficient to highlight the algorithmic trade-off between Energy and Valence.

The Grand Finale: What the Data Taught Us

Our investigation reveals that Spotify’s genre labels are often functional physical markers rather than true emotional reflections.

The "Happy" tag is the ultimate paradox: it represents songs that are nearly twice as energetic as average music but tonally 31.7% less positive.

Meanwhile, "Sad" acts as a reliable catch-all for stillness, defined more by an 11% drop in energy than actual emotional misery.

The final verdict is that Spotify’s ecosystem prioritises intensity over sentiment, effectively treating "Happy" as fast and "Sad" as quiet.

To truly curate your mood, look past human labels and trust underlying metrics like Energy and Valence. The data doesn’t change how the music feels, but it fundamentally changes how we understand the vibe.

Don’t just listen to the label; listen to the data.
